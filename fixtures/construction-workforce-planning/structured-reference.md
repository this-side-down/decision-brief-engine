# Construction workforce planning structured reference

## Context

The team needs to decide whether to explore specialty trade workforce planning before the Q4 planning cycle or keep the roadmap focused on GC workforce planning.

Several specialty contractors are asking for better visibility into foreman, superintendent, PM, and self-perform crew availability across jobs. Most are currently using spreadsheets, weekly staffing calls, and judgment from operations leaders.

GC customers are getting value from project-level staffing visibility, especially when they can see gaps 6–12 months ahead. The team does not want to muddy that workflow or create a half-built specialty contractor experience.

Sales believes specialty trades could expand the market from GC workforce planning into broader people planning for construction. Product and design are concerned that trade workflows may require different concepts than the current model, including crews, certifications, union rules, geography, short-notice callouts, and field leadership planning.

The current product model is project/person/role oriented, not crew oriented.

## Decision to make

Should the team run a narrow specialty trade workforce planning pilot before Q4, or stay focused on the existing GC workforce planning roadmap?

## Options

1. Pilot specialty trade forecasting with three design partners in August.
2. Stay focused on GC workforce planning until the current roadmap is stronger.
3. Build a lightweight discovery prototype for specialty trades without committing to production delivery.
4. Add trade-specific fields to the current product and test whether the existing workflow stretches far enough.

## Stakeholders

- Product and design
- Sales
- Customer success
- Engineering
- Executive team
- VP Ops at a large mechanical contractor
- Workforce planning lead at an electrical contractor
- Potential self-perform or concrete trade partner
- Existing GC customers

## Goals

- Validate whether specialty trades are a real near-term expansion path.
- Avoid overfitting the product to one trade partner.
- Preserve momentum with GC customers.
- Learn whether crew-level planning belongs in the core product.
- Support sales with a credible story without selling vaporware.
- Keep the pilot small enough for engineering to support.
- Understand whether specialty trades can extend the current platform or require a separate product path.

## Risks

- Specialty trade workflows may be too different from GC workforce planning.
- Crew-level planning could require deeper data model changes than expected.
- Pilot customers may expect production-ready functionality.
- Sales may position the pilot as a committed roadmap item.
- Engineering could get pulled into one-off fields or custom workflows.
- GC roadmap work could slip.
- A narrow pilot may produce learnings that do not generalize.

## Constraints

- Engineering has about one sprint of discovery or prototype capacity before Q4 planning.
- Design can support interviews and workflow mapping, but not a full new product surface.
- Sales wants something credible to discuss with prospects this quarter.
- Customer success does not want to support a half-built workflow across too many accounts.
- The team does not yet know whether certifications, unions, or regional labor rules are must-haves.
- The existing data model is project/person/role oriented, not crew oriented.

## Evidence

- Two expansion conversations mentioned trade contractor workforce visibility.
- A mechanical contractor said late senior foreman assignment hurts margin.
- An electrical contractor said spreadsheet staffing reviews already happen every Friday.
- GC customers mostly plan around salaried project teams.
- Trade contractors may plan around crews and field leadership.
- Sales says this could support a broader “people planning for construction” story.
- Engineering says adding a few fields is easy, but changing crew planning logic is hard.

## Open questions

- Do specialty trades need crew-level planning on day one, or is role/person forecasting enough?
- Which trade segment should be the first design partner: mechanical, electrical, concrete, or self-perform?
- Can sales frame the pilot as research without creating delivery expectations?
- What is the minimum useful workflow for a VP Ops?
- Are certifications, unions, and regional labor rules core requirements or edge cases?
- Would the same forecasting views work for both GCs and specialty trades?
- Who owns pilot success criteria: product, sales, or customer success?

## Tensions

- Sales wants to move quickly, while product wants stronger validation.
- Specialty trades are strategically attractive, but may pull the product away from the GC workflow.
- A small pilot could generate useful learning, but could also create custom-work expectations.
- Waiting preserves roadmap focus, but may miss a market-learning window.
- Adding fields is easy, but validating a repeatable workflow is harder.

## Current leaning

Run a narrow discovery pilot with three specialty trade design partners across at least two trade types.

Do not commit to production roadmap delivery yet. Test whether role/person-level forecasting is useful before investing in crew-level planning. Sales can reference the pilot as research or a design partner effort, not as a committed feature.

## Proposed next steps

- Identify three design partners across at least two trade types.
- Define pilot success criteria before build work starts.
- Run workflow interviews with VP Ops and staffing coordinators.
- Map where the current GC workforce planning model fits or breaks.
- Create a prototype or mocked workflow before changing the production data model.
- Align with executives on sales language.
- Decide by the end of the pilot whether specialty trades extend the current platform or require a separate product path.
